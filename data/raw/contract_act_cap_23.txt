LAW OF CONTRACT ACT
CHAPTER 23 OF THE LAWS OF KENYA
[Placeholder legal corpus for RAG development]

PART I — PRELIMINARY

1. Short title
This Act may be cited as the Law of Contract Act.

2. Application of English law of contract
Subject to any written law, the common law of England relating to contract, as modified by
the doctrines of equity, shall extend and apply to all contracts made in Kenya.

PART II — FORMATION OF CONTRACTS

3. Requirements for written contracts
(1) A contract for the sale or other disposition of an interest in land shall be in writing,
signed by or on behalf of each party.

(2) Writing is also required for any contract which expressly states that it must be in writing.

(3) Where a contract for the sale of an interest in land is not in writing signed by the parties,
it shall not be enforceable in an action unless there has been sufficient part performance of
the contract by the party seeking to enforce it, or unless the court is satisfied that it would
be inequitable for the party against whom enforcement is sought to deny the existence of the
contract.

(4) Part performance includes payment of deposit, taking possession of land, making improvements,
or any other act done by one party in reliance on the contract with the knowledge of the other party.

4. Offer and acceptance
(1) An offer is a proposal made by one party to another with the intention that it shall become
binding on acceptance.

(2) Acceptance must be absolute and unqualified. A conditional acceptance or counter-offer is
a rejection of the original offer and constitutes a new offer.

(3) An offer may be revoked at any time before acceptance, provided such revocation is communicated
to the offeree before acceptance.

5. Consideration
(1) A promise made without consideration is not enforceable unless it is made by deed.

(2) Consideration may be executed (an act performed at the time of the contract) or executory
(a promise to perform an act in the future).

(3) Past consideration is not good consideration in law.

(4) Consideration need not be adequate but must be real and of some value in the eyes of the law.

6. Capacity to contract
(1) Every person is competent to contract who is of the age of majority (18 years) and who is
of sound mind.

(2) A minor is not bound by a contract except for necessaries.

(3) A person of unsound mind is not bound by a contract unless it is shown that the other party
was unaware of the incapacity.

PART III — PERFORMANCE AND BREACH

7. Obligations of parties
(1) Each party to a contract is bound to perform their obligations as agreed.

(2) Where one party fails to perform, the other party may treat the contract as discharged if
the breach goes to the root of the contract (fundamental breach).

8. Discharge by breach
(1) A contract may be discharged by breach where one party refuses or fails to perform their
contractual obligations.

(2) Where a party repudiates a contract before the time of performance, the other party may
accept the repudiation and sue immediately for damages (anticipatory breach).

9. Damages for breach
(1) Where a contract is breached, the innocent party is entitled to recover damages.

(2) Damages are assessed at the time of breach and are intended to place the innocent party in
the position they would have been in had the contract been performed (expectation measure).

(3) The claimant has a duty to mitigate their loss by taking reasonable steps to reduce the
consequences of the breach.

(4) Remote or unforeseeable losses are not recoverable: Hadley v Baxendale principle.

PART IV — REMEDIES

10. Specific performance
(1) Specific performance is an equitable remedy requiring the party in breach to perform their
contractual obligations.

(2) Specific performance will generally be granted where damages are an inadequate remedy,
particularly in contracts for the sale of land (since land is considered unique).

(3) The court will not grant specific performance where:
    (a) the party seeking it has not performed or offered to perform their own obligations;
    (b) the contract was induced by fraud, misrepresentation or mistake;
    (c) specific performance would cause undue hardship.

11. Rescission
(1) Rescission sets aside the contract and restores the parties to their pre-contractual positions.

(2) Rescission may be claimed for misrepresentation, duress, undue influence, or mistake.

12. Injunctions
An injunction may be granted to restrain a party from committing or continuing a breach of contract.

PART V — SPECIFIC TYPES OF CONTRACTS

13. Contracts for the sale of goods
(1) The Sale of Goods Act governs contracts for the sale of goods.

(2) In contracts for the sale of goods, property and risk pass as agreed by the parties or
according to the rules set out in the Sale of Goods Act.

14. Contracts for services
(1) A party providing services under a contract is bound to exercise the standard of care and
skill reasonably expected of a person in that profession or trade.

(2) Implied terms in contracts for services include: reasonable care and skill, reasonable time
for completion, and a reasonable price if not specified.

15. Agency contracts
(1) An agent has authority to act on behalf of the principal to the extent of their actual or
ostensible authority.

(2) The principal is bound by contracts made by the agent within the scope of their authority.

PART VI — ILLEGALITY AND PUBLIC POLICY

16. Illegal contracts
(1) A contract is illegal where its formation, purpose, or performance is prohibited by statute
or is contrary to public policy.

(2) An illegal contract is void and unenforceable. No action can be brought on such a contract.

(3) Where parties are not in pari delicto (equal fault), the less culpable party may recover
money paid or property transferred.

17. Restraint of trade
(1) A covenant in restraint of trade is prima facie void but may be upheld if it is reasonable
in the interests of the parties and of the public.

(2) In employment contracts, a restraint must be reasonable as to its geographic scope, duration,
and subject matter.
