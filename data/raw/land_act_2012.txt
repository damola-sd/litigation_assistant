PART I — PRELIMINARY

1. Short title and commencement
This Act may be cited as the Land Act, 2012.

2. Interpretation
In this Act, unless the context otherwise requires:
"land" includes the surface of the earth, anything below the surface, anything above the surface,
and any interest, right, or other benefit arising out of land.
"community land" means land lawfully held, managed and used by a specific community.
"private land" means registered land held by any person under a freehold tenure, or leasehold tenure.
"public land" means land as defined in Article 62 of the Constitution.

PART II — GENERAL PRINCIPLES

3. Rights and interests in land
(1) Every person has the right to acquire and own land anywhere in Kenya, either individually
or in association with others, subject to the Constitution and this Act.

(2) Land ownership is not absolute and may be subject to restrictions imposed for public interest
purposes including planning, environmental protection, and other public purposes.

4. Forms of tenure
Land in Kenya is held under the following tenures:
(a) freehold;
(b) leasehold;
(c) such forms of tenure as may be recognised under the Community Land Act, 2016.

PART III — TRANSACTIONS IN LAND

5. Prohibition on transactions without consent
(1) No transaction in land shall be effected unless the transaction is in accordance with this Act.

(2) Any transaction that purports to alienate land in a manner not permitted by this Act is void.

6. Agreements for sale of land
(1) Every agreement for the sale or other disposition of an interest in land shall be in writing
signed by the vendor and the purchaser or their duly authorised agents.

(2) An agreement for sale shall specify:
    (a) the parties to the transaction;
    (b) the land to be sold or disposed of;
    (c) the purchase price or consideration;
    (d) the time for completion of the transaction.

(3) The vendor shall furnish the purchaser with all documents of title relating to the land.

7. Deposits and part payment
(1) On signing an agreement for sale, the purchaser may be required to pay a deposit, which
shall not ordinarily exceed ten percent of the purchase price.

(2) Where a purchaser has paid a deposit and entered into possession of land, the vendor shall
not deny the existence of the contract.

(3) Part performance of an agreement for sale, including payment of the purchase price or a
substantial portion thereof, taking possession, or carrying out improvements on the land with
the knowledge of the vendor, entitles the purchaser to enforce the agreement by way of specific
performance.

PART IV — TRANSFER OF LAND

8. How land is transferred
(1) An interest in land shall be transferred by a transfer registered in accordance with the
Land Registration Act, 2012.

(2) Upon execution of the agreement for sale, the vendor is under an obligation to execute a
transfer in favour of the purchaser within a reasonable time.

(3) Where a vendor refuses or fails to execute the transfer documents, the purchaser may apply
to the court for an order of specific performance.

9. Completion of sale
(1) Upon payment of the full purchase price, the vendor shall:
    (a) execute all necessary transfer documents;
    (b) deliver all title documents to the purchaser;
    (c) cooperate fully in the registration of the transfer.

(2) Any failure by the vendor to execute transfer documents after receiving full consideration
constitutes a fundamental breach of the agreement entitling the purchaser to specific performance
and/or damages.

PART V — SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE AND REMEDIES

38. Specific performance of agreements for sale of land
(1) Where a vendor or lessor fails or refuses to perform their obligations under an agreement
for sale or lease, the court may, on an application by the purchaser or lessee, make an order
of specific performance requiring the vendor or lessor to complete the transaction.

(2) In determining whether to grant specific performance, the court shall consider:
    (a) whether the purchaser has performed or is ready to perform their obligations;
    (b) the nature and extent of the breach;
    (c) any hardship that would be caused to either party;
    (d) whether damages would be an adequate remedy.

(3) The court shall generally grant specific performance in land transactions because land is
regarded as unique and damages are ordinarily an inadequate remedy.

(4) Part performance by the purchaser — including payment of all or a substantial portion of
the purchase price, taking possession, or making improvements — is sufficient grounds for the
court to order specific performance even in the absence of a written agreement in certain circumstances.

39. Remedies for breach of land transaction
(1) Where a party to a land transaction is in breach, the innocent party may:
    (a) sue for specific performance;
    (b) rescind the agreement and recover any money paid;
    (c) claim damages for loss suffered as a result of the breach.

(2) These remedies may be cumulative except where they are inconsistent.

PART VI — COMPULSORY ACQUISITION

40. Power of compulsory acquisition
(1) The national and county governments may acquire land compulsorily for public purposes
subject to the Constitution and applicable law.

(2) Prompt payment of just compensation shall be made to every person whose land is
compulsorily acquired.

PART VII — LAND ADMINISTRATION AND MANAGEMENT

45. Role of the National Land Commission
(1) The National Land Commission shall administer all public land on behalf of the national
and county governments.

(2) The Commission shall investigate present and historical land injustices and recommend
appropriate redress.

50. Land use planning
(1) All land in Kenya shall be used in accordance with applicable land use plans.

(2) Development permission is required for any use of land that is contrary to the designated
use under the applicable land use plan.

PART VIII — MISCELLANEOUS

60. Offences
(1) Any person who fraudulently transfers land or procures the registration of a transfer
by fraud commits an offence and is liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding ten million
shillings or imprisonment for a term not exceeding ten years or both.

(2) Fraud includes misrepresentation, concealment of material facts, or any other conduct
designed to deceive another party in relation to a land transaction.
